Direct and Indirect Speech (Narration)

📘 What is Narration? Narration is the art of reporting someone's words. There are two ways: Direct Speech (exact words) and Indirect Speech (reported words).

1. Introduction

  • Direct Speech: The exact words spoken, enclosed in quotation marks.
    Rohan said, "I am busy."
  • Indirect Speech (Reported Speech): Reporting without quoting the exact words, with changes in tense, pronouns, and other words.
    Rohan said that he was busy.

2. Basic Rules for Conversion

A. Change of Tenses

If the reporting verb (e.g., said, told) is in the past tense, the tense of the reported speech usually changes as follows:

Direct Tense Indirect Tense Example
Simple Present Simple Past "I eat apples." → He said he ate apples.
Present Continuous Past Continuous "I am eating." → He said he was eating.
Present Perfect Past Perfect "I have eaten." → He said he had eaten.
Simple Past Past Perfect "I ate." → He said he had eaten.
Past Continuous Past Perfect Continuous "I was eating." → He said he had been eating.
Past Perfect Past Perfect (no change) "I had eaten." → He said he had eaten.
Simple Future (will) Future in Past (would) "I will eat." → He said he would eat.
Future Perfect Future Perfect in Past "I will have eaten." → He said he would have eaten.

Exceptions: Universal truths, habitual facts, or historical facts remain unchanged.
He said, "The sun rises in the east." → He said that the sun rises in the east.

B. Change of Pronouns

Pronouns change according to the subject and object of the reporting verb.

Direct Pronoun Change Rule Example
I, we, me, us, my, our According to the subject of reporting verb He said, "I am tired." → He said that he was tired.
you, your, yours According to the object of reporting verb He said to me, "You are late." → He told me that I was late.
he, she, they, etc. Usually no change (but can change depending on context) He said, "She is nice." → He said that she was nice.

C. Change of Words (Adverbs of Place, Time, and Demonstratives)

Direct Indirect
now then
here there
today that day
yesterday the previous day / the day before
tomorrow the next day / the following day
last week the previous week / the week before
next week the following week / the week after
ago before
this that
these those
thus so

Note: These changes may not apply if the reporting happens in the same place/time (e.g., "I am here now" said in the same place – may keep "here").

3. Conversion for Different Types of Sentences

A. Assertive Sentences

  • Use conjunction "that" (often omitted in informal speech).
  • Reporting verbs: said, told, informed, replied, etc.
  • He said, "I am happy." → He said that he was happy.

B. Interrogative Sentences

  • Yes/No Questions: Use if or whether. Change the word order to statement form.
    He said to me, "Do you like coffee?" → He asked me if I liked coffee.
  • Wh- Questions: Use the wh-word itself as the conjunction. Change word order to statement.
    She asked, "Where do you live?" → She asked where I lived.
  • Reporting verbs: asked, inquired, questioned, wanted to know.
  • Do not use "that" after the reporting verb.

C. Imperative Sentences (Orders, Requests, Advice, Suggestions)

  • Use to-infinitive (to + V1) for commands/requests.
  • Reporting verbs: ordered, commanded, requested, begged, advised, suggested, proposed, etc.
  • Negative imperatives: Use not to + V1.
  • He said, "Please sit down." → He requested me to sit down.
  • The teacher said, "Don't talk." → The teacher forbade us to talk. OR The teacher ordered us not to talk.
  • Special verbs like "suggest": For suggestions, sometimes use "suggest + that + clause" with "should".
    He said, "Let's go to the park." → He suggested that we should go to the park. OR He suggested going to the park.

D. Exclamatory Sentences

  • Convert into an assertive sentence expressing the emotion.
  • Use reporting verbs like exclaimed with joy/sorrow, cried out, applauded, etc.
  • He said, "What a beautiful scene!" → He exclaimed that it was a very beautiful scene.
  • She said, "Alas! I am ruined." → She exclaimed with sorrow that she was ruined.

E. Optative Sentences (Wishes, Prayers, Curses)

  • Use "wished" or "prayed".
  • Use "that" and change the verb to past form (or "might").
  • He said, "May you live long!" → He wished that I might live long.
  • She said, "Would that I were rich!" → She wished that she had been rich.

4. Special Cases and Tricky Questions

A. Sentences with "Let"

"Let" can be used in different senses: proposal, permission, suggestion.

  • Proposal (including the speaker): Use "suggested that we should" or "proposed + -ing".
    He said, "Let us go for a walk." → He suggested that they should go for a walk. (If "us" includes the listener, may use "we").
  • Permission (allowing someone to do something): Use "said that + clause" or "allowed".
    He said, "Let him play." → He said that he might be allowed to play. OR He allowed him to play.
  • Challenge/Indifference: Use "defied" or "challenged".
    He said, "Let him try." → He challenged him to try.

B. Sentences with "Would that" (Optative)

He said, "Would that I were young again!" → He wished that he had been young again.

C. Sentences with "Good morning", "Thank you", etc. (Exclamations/Greetings)

These are not reported in the usual way; we use verbs like wished, greeted, thanked, bade.

He said, "Good morning, sir." → He wished/saluted the gentleman good morning. OR He greeted the gentleman.
She said, "Thank you." → She thanked me. (Object may be added)

D. Mixed Sentences (Combination of Types)

He said, "It is raining, so take an umbrella." → He said that it was raining and advised me to take an umbrella.

E. Sentences with "Needn't" / "Must"

  • "Must" usually changes to "had to" or remains "must" if it expresses a command/necessity in the past.
    He said, "You must work hard." → He said that I had to work hard. OR He said that I must work hard (if it's still relevant).
  • "Needn't" (absence of necessity) changes to "didn't need to" or "didn't have to".
    He said, "You needn't come." → He said that I didn't need to come. OR He said that I didn't have to come.

F. Sentences with "Should" (Advice, Moral obligation)

Often remains "should" if it expresses advice or moral duty.

He said, "You should respect your elders." → He said that I should respect my elders.

G. Sentences with "Sir", "Madam" as address

These are omitted in indirect speech, but the respect can be conveyed by using appropriate reporting verb (e.g., addressed respectfully).

5. Common Errors to Avoid

  • Forgetting to change the pronoun according to the context.
  • Not changing the tense when the reporting verb is past.
  • Using "that" in questions.
  • Changing universal truths.
  • Wrong conversion of "let" or "would that".

6. Practice Questions (Tricky Ones)

  1. He said, "What a pity you didn't come!"
  2. She said to me, "Please wait here till I return."
  3. The teacher said, "Don't make a noise."
  4. He said, "Let us wait for the result."
  5. My mother said, "May God bless you!"
  6. He said, "Would that I were a bird!"
  7. The stranger said, "Which is the way to the station?"
  8. He said, "Goodbye, my friends."
  9. She said, "I am going to London tomorrow."
  10. He said to her, "Do you know where Rahul lives?"
  11. "Alas! I have lost my purse," she cried.
  12. He said, "The earth moves round the sun."
  13. I said to him, "Will you help me if I need you?"
  14. He said, "I must go now."
  15. She said, "Let him say whatever he likes."
  16. He said, "Happy Diwali!" to his neighbours.
  17. He said, "How well she sings!"
  18. The officer said to the clerk, "See that the letters are typed."
  19. He said, "I can swim across the river."
  20. She said, "We used to live in Delhi."

Answers

  1. He exclaimed with pity that I had not come. OR He exclaimed that it was a great pity that I had not come.
  2. She requested me to wait there till she returned.
  3. The teacher forbade us to make a noise. / The teacher ordered us not to make a noise.
  4. He suggested that they should wait for the result. / He proposed waiting for the result.
  5. My mother prayed that God might bless me.
  6. He wished that he had been a bird.
  7. The stranger asked which the way to the station was. / The stranger asked which was the way to the station.
  8. He bade his friends goodbye.
  9. She said that she was going to London the next day.
  10. He asked her if she knew where Rahul lived.
  11. She exclaimed with sorrow that she had lost her purse.
  12. He said that the earth moves round the sun. (Universal truth)
  13. I asked him if he would help me if I needed him.
  14. He said that he had to go then. / He said that he must go then. (if still relevant)
  15. She said that he might be allowed to say whatever he liked. / She allowed him to say whatever he liked.
  16. He wished his neighbours a happy Diwali.
  17. He exclaimed that she sang very well.
  18. The officer ordered the clerk to see that the letters were typed.
  19. He said that he could swim across the river.
  20. She said that they used to live in Delhi.
💡 Pro Tip: Always identify the type of sentence first (assertive, interrogative, imperative, exclamatory, optative) and then apply the corresponding rules. Practice mixed sentences to master narration.
